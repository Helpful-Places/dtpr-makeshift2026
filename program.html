Building the Accountability Layer for Public AI: Methods, Standards, and the Communities They Serve

Meg Young, Jake Hirsch-Allen, Jacqueline Lu Moderator: Dr. Pamela Robinson

A growing movement argues that AI deployed in the public interest must be built as public infrastructure - with public access, public accountability, and permanence as a public good. But what does accountability *actually look like* in practice? Who participates, at what stage, and through what mechanisms?

This panel brings together researchers and practitioners working on different pieces of this puzzle. From the Public AI Network's vision for AI as civic infrastructure, to Data & Society's pioneering work on community-based algorithmic impact assessment, to the DTPR open standard deployed in 15+ cities as a "legibility layer" for urban AI, to a live Canadian project building national consensus on AI interpretability - the panelists have each confronted the same core question from different angles: how do you give communities genuine agency over the AI systems that shape public life?

The conversation moves from vision to methods to infrastructure to norms, surfacing both what's working and where participatory approaches hit real limits.

You Tracked My What? Can responsive spaces show, not tell how they work?

Spatial Pixel (Violet Whitney, William Martin)

In responsive environments, accountability can’t rely only on signs, labels, or disclosures. People need to form a working mental model of the system through the behavior of the space itself: what it responds to, where its boundaries are, how cause and effect works, and how they can change it.

We explore how responsive environments can make themselves understandable through interaction rather than static disclosure alone. Using the Spatial Pixel Lab at SVA as a case study, the talk and demo asks how people form mental models of responsive environments. Can they see what the space senses? Can they understand cause and effect? Can they tell when a behavior starts and stops? Can they change the system without becoming programmers?

In a future where spaces can be authored in real time, the most trustworthy spaces may be those that teach people how they work.

From content curation to automated decision-making, algorithmic systems have become ubiquitous in mediating human interactions. SMOL GARDENS: Accountable AI for Civic Tech

Femmecubator (Krizia Fernando, Priyanka Thomas, Kris Rubiano)

The workshop begins with a short presentation on the Smol Gardens project. The talk will also focus on the current state of AI-enabled web design, a self-auditing impact framework, and small language models as a good alternative to LLMs. The workshop will introduce design principles and an approach to building civic tech grounded in continuous accountability to affected communities, transparency about tradeoffs, and openness to critique to strengthen community agency and democratic self-governance.

In the hands-on portion, participants will apply the framework by vibecoding a simple AI application using a small language model and evaluating its impact in three core areas: human and societal impact, future systems, and environmental responsibility. This workshop is for designers, builders and civic technologists who are already using AI tools in their work but feel caught between the pressure to move fast and the need to build responsibly.

Smol Gardens is an initiative by the Femmecubator team, introduced at BetaNYC's UnSchool of Data 2026 conference, and pitched as part of the Open Civic Tech initiative.

Designing Infrastructures Not Interfaces

Panelists: Marco Zamarato, Pinar Guvenc, Adriana Valdez Young, Moderator: Georgia Bullen

From Control to Complexity examines the history of systems thinking to argue for a fundamental shift in how we understand the designer's role. From early reductionism through cybernetics, chaos theory, and complex adaptive systems, the session traces how our models of the world evolved and why design practice hasn't kept up. In a post-product reality where the things we design are entangled, prismatic, and ungovernable by any single actor, the solution-based model of design thinking has reached its limits. If we are no longer building products but infrastructures, the designer's role shifts from solver of discrete problems to steward, facilitator, and steerer of complex systems.

The talk maps how this understanding developed: from the clockwork predictability of Newtonian thinking, through the feedback loops of cybernetics and Stafford Beer's ambitious experiments in governance, to the humbling lessons of chaos theory and emergence. It grounds core concepts like self-organization, feedback, leverage points, modularity, and edge-of-chaos dynamics in design practice, drawing on soft systems methodology, participatory design, and examples from my own work designing AI systems, privacy architectures, and governance frameworks across 17 years at Google and now through Under Over Through, a research and production studio working at the intersection of AI, design, and governance.

What if technology honoured beauty instead of optimizing it away? An Inquiry through Kolams and Islamic interlace patterns

Lavannya Suressh

If technology optimizes everything, why does the world feel less beautiful? The hunger for beauty was more apparent in the past. Across cultures and centuries, pattern systems like Islamic interlace, Celtic knots, and Kolams each reflect distinct ways of translating meaning through repetition, symmetry, and rule-based systems into visual form. This session uses Kolams, a South Indian art form, to explore the algorithmic logic behind pattern systems. By recognizing cultural patterns as living systems of knowledge, we can engage with them as collaborators to extend their logic into new, expressive forms supporting a notion of technological progress that doesn't abandon beauty.

The Internet's Urban Planning Problem

Keelin Burke

Urban planning can make or break a city. What if we viewed digital design the same way? The shift from citizen to consumer didn't start with the internet, but digital design pushed it into overdrive. Algorithms and platform decisions are made entirely outside of public awareness, intentionally crafted to steer users below the threshold of awareness toward the most profitable experience. Dark patterns and an ever-expanding digital panopticon have quietly eroded political efficacy in ways we're only beginning to understand. Marketers and advertisers are calling a lot of these shots. And most don't fully understand the terms.

This session will explore the above and close with an open discussion. Participants will walk away with a deeper understanding of the relationship between digital design, consumerism, and political efficacy.

3:30pm - 4:00pm Plenary

More Interestingly Less Wrong: frameworks for municipal AI experimentation

Iain Montgomery

Most people in cities aren't technologists or policy wonks. They sit somewhere between cautious optimism and quiet fear about AI, but nobody's really talking to them. Meanwhile, the people designing AI governance frameworks are arguing with each other in a language most of the city doesn't speak, about solutions most of the city didn't actually ask for. This is a design problem, more than a communication one.

This session will start with a provocation that the best models for introducing genuinely new things into complex systems probably aren't in the smart cities literature. They're in jazz, pirate codes or the kink community's consent frameworks, as well as how Lego thinks about creative constraint. These are serious, skilled, high-stakes, deeply intentional practitioners who contribute cultures of structured experimentation and psychological safety around being wrong. Not ""failing fast"", but being 'more interestingly less wrong'.

Municipal AI might be doing the opposite, as cities import ""best practice"" solutions because someone else did them, then layer them on top of existing assumptions and call it innovation. The accountability conversation consistently arrives after the infrastructure is embedded and a vendor contract has been signed. It means we're designing legibility standards for systems we haven't yet decided what to do with.

The session will shift from provocative talk to become more of a facilitated salon to explore what would it actually mean to design a culture of structured experimentation around AI in cities, before the framework rather than after. So what can DTPR learn from communities that got good at being wrong in public?

Participants leave with a draft provocation contributed back to the DTPR community as a ""more interestingly less wrong"" framework for municipal AI experimentation.
